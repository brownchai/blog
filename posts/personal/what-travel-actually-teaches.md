I used to believe travel made you more open-minded. I've revised this. Travel makes you more aware of how much you assumed.

The difference matters.

## The inventory of assumptions

The first time I took a train alone in a country whose language I don't speak, I discovered how many things I'd been doing on autopilot. Buying food, navigating, asking for help — all these tasks I'd thought of as simple turned out to be simple only because I'd built up invisible scaffolding over years of repetition.

Without the scaffolding, I had to think again.

This is uncomfortable in the moment and clarifying in retrospect. You see the shape of your assumptions when they stop working.

## What you don't learn from travel

You don't automatically learn empathy. You don't automatically learn to see the world through different eyes. Tourists can travel constantly and return home entirely unchanged.

What you get from travel is *material* — the experience of disorientation, of things not working the way you expect, of having to improvise. What you do with that material is up to you.

Some people use the disorientation as confirmation of home's superiority. Some people find it genuinely interesting. The disposition matters more than the miles.

## The useful kind of lost

The best travel experiences I've had involved being slightly too lost, in a city where I couldn't immediately fix it with my phone. Not dangerously lost — just inconveniently so. Stuck enough that I had to interact, ask, observe.

There's a version of travel that insulates you from this: the pre-booked, app-navigated, Tripadvisor-reviewed version. It's not bad. But it's essentially tourism with better logistics. The texture of a place doesn't live in the famous things.

## What I actually remember

Looking back at the places I've been, what I remember is rarely the landmark.

I remember a breakfast counter in Lisbon where no one spoke English and the whole transaction was conducted in mime and goodwill. A train delay in Japan that ended with a stranger drawing me a map by hand. Getting thoroughly rained on in Edinburgh and deciding to find a pub and stay there for three hours.

None of this was planned. All of it taught me something.

I've started thinking of discomfort as the actual product. The photo is nice. The discomfort is the education.
