## Day One — Arrival and First Impressions
*March 19, 2026*

The wheels touched down at Tribhuvan International Airport at half past one in the afternoon. Kathmandu announced itself before we'd even cleared arrivals — a plate of steaming momos from an airport stall, the dumplings plump and slick with chilli oil, the kind of welcome only Nepal knows how to give.

A cab wound its way through the organised chaos of the city and deposited us in Thamel, that dense, electric neighbourhood that has served as base camp for travellers for decades. We checked into the Timber House Hotel, dropped our bags, and headed straight back out. Kathmandu doesn't reward those who rest.

### Boudhanath at Golden Hour

Our first real destination was Boudhanath Stupa — that colossal white dome topped with the Buddha's all-seeing eyes, prayer flags radiating outward like the spokes of a spiritual wheel. There's a gravity to the place that slows you down. Pilgrims circled the base in quiet, clockwise procession. We joined them, briefly, before hunger pulled us upward.

Rosemary Kitchen & Coffee Shop — perched on a rooftop overlooking the stupa — turned out to be the perfect first-evening haunt. We ordered Gorkha beers and chicken sadheko, the spiced meat bright with mustard oil and fresh lime. From our table, the stupa filled the entire frame — white dome, golden spire, a sky turning amber. It was the kind of view that makes you put your phone down and just sit with it for a while.

### Thamel After Dark

Back in Thamel, we went looking for noise — the good kind. Fat Monk delivered. A live band worked through a setlist that had the room swaying, and we settled in for a long evening. Pork choila arrived first — smoky, charred, and fiercely spiced — followed by chicken spring rolls, garlic potato wedges with a satisfying crunch, and a bowl of chicken chow mein that we didn't need but couldn't refuse. Yeti Beer washed it all down, cold and crisp, the unofficial fuel of any Kathmandu night out.

We walked back to the hotel through Thamel's narrowing lanes, the neon signs flickering overhead, music leaking from half a dozen doorways. Day one, and Nepal had already set the tone.

## Day Two — Temples, Monkeys, and a Hailstorm
*March 20, 2026*

### Morning Fuel

The day began at Mitho Café, a quiet spot where we eased into the morning properly. A double-shot espresso for me, a cappuccino for Shriya, and a slice of apple pie that deserves its own paragraph — the thing was more apple than pastry, stuffed thick and warm, with a crumble that gave way to soft, cinnamon-laced fruit. The kitchen sent out complimentary brownie pieces, still slightly warm. An auspicious start.

### Durbar Square and the Living Goddess

Kathmandu's Durbar Square is the kind of place that overwhelms you in the best way. We hired a guide — the right call — and spent the next couple of hours moving between ancient temples, each one layered with woodcarving so intricate it felt like staring at a language you almost understand. The architecture here is Newari at its finest: pagoda roofs stacked three and four tiers high, erotic carvings tucked into the struts, courtyards that have witnessed coronations and earthquakes in equal measure.

The highlight, though, was the Kumari — Kathmandu's living goddess. Our guide walked us through the tradition: a young girl from the Shakya clan, selected through an elaborate series of rituals, who serves as the embodiment of the goddess Taleju until she reaches puberty. We waited in the courtyard of the Kumari Ghar, and then — briefly, silently — she appeared at the window. A child's face framed in dark carved wood, ancient and immediate all at once. No photographs allowed. Some things you just hold in memory.

### Swayambhunath and the Cost of a Coke

From there, we climbed to Swayambhunath — the Monkey Temple — perched on a hilltop west of the city. The name earns itself within minutes. Monkeys rule this place with a confidence that borders on extortion. I purchased a bottle of Coke at the base of the steps. A macaque relieved me of it before I'd taken a single sip — snatched clean from my hand with a speed that suggested extensive practice. I wasn't even angry. Respect where it's due.

### The Chandragiri Detour

We had originally planned a straight cab ride to Bhaktapur, but somewhere along the ring road, ambition got the better of us. Chandragiri Hills — why not? We rerouted, reached the cable car station, and ascended.

The ride up was worth the detour alone — the Kathmandu Valley spreading out below like a living map, the city giving way to terraced hills and, on a clear day, the Himalayan range on the horizon. At the summit, we found jhol momos — dumplings swimming in a spicy, tangy soup broth — and a plate of chicken chow mein. The momos were outstanding, the kind that make you close your eyes on the first bite. Simple food, extraordinary setting.

### Hailstorm and a Rooftop Retreat

We descended and pointed the cab toward Bhaktapur, but Nepal had other plans. A hailstorm rolled in — sudden and theatrical, the sky turning charcoal, ice rattling off the windshield like applause. We called it. Bhaktapur would wait.

Back at the Timber House, we climbed to the rooftop and made the most of the downtime. Sherpa Beer and apple cider in hand, we watched the storm soften into drizzle over Thamel's rooftops. Peanut sadheko — roasted peanuts tossed with onion, chilli, and a squeeze of lime — and crispy corn kept us honest. Sometimes the best travel moments are the unplanned ones: a rooftop, a beer, and a city humming quietly below.

### Newari Food and a Friday Night in Thamel

By eight, the rain had stopped and we were restless. The plan was simple: find proper Newari food — the indigenous cuisine of the Kathmandu Valley that most tourist restaurants skip over entirely.

Pasa Newari Kitchen delivered. We started with buff and cheese chatamari — often described as a Newari pizza, though that undersells it. A thin rice-flour crepe spread wide on a hot griddle, topped with minced buffalo meat and melted cheese, the edges crisp and lacy, the centre soft and savoury. It's the kind of dish that makes you wonder why it isn't famous worldwide. We followed it with a chicken kana set — the traditional Newari thali — an elaborate spread of beaten rice, marinated meat, achar, dal, and greens, each element distinct, the plate a small education in how the Newars eat.

For dessert, we wandered over to Pumpernickel Bakery and split a slice of pistachio cake — dense, fragrant, not too sweet. The right way to end a meal that heavy.

Outside, Thamel had come alive. The rain had washed the streets clean and the neighbourhood glowed — fairy lights strung between buildings, shopfronts spilling warm light onto wet pavement, clusters of people drifting between bars and restaurants. Friday night energy, unmistakable and infectious. We passed Purple Haze on the walk back — a rock bar with live music thumping through the doors, the kind of place that pulls you in by the bass line. We made a mental note: Sunday evening, when we're back from Pokhara, that's the stop.

Tomorrow, we fly west. Pokhara — lakes, mountains, and a different Nepal altogether. The bags are half-packed, the alarm is set, and Kathmandu has left us hungry for more.

## Day Three — Westward, to Pokhara
*March 21, 2026*

### The 6:30 Checkout

We left the Timber House at half six, the streets of Thamel still half-asleep — shutters down, stray dogs curled in doorways, the occasional early-rising shopkeeper hosing down the pavement. Our cab driver was a familiar face: the same man who'd taken us to Chandragiri the day before, a friendly guy with good instincts. He'd told us not to rush to the airport — check-in only opens an hour before departure, he said. He was right. We were early by a comfortable margin and spent the excess time the way airports demand: bad coffee, a book, and the particular restlessness of waiting for a short flight.

### Twenty-Five Minutes in the Clouds

The flight took off at 9:40 and landed in Pokhara at 10:05. Twenty-five minutes gate to gate — barely enough time to settle into your seat before the descent begins. We'd done our homework and booked the left side of the aircraft, the side that's supposed to reward you with an unbroken panorama of the Annapurna range, Machhapuchhre's fishtail peak, and the white spine of the Himalayas stretching into the distance.

The weather had other ideas. A thick quilt of cloud hung low over the valley, and the mountains stayed hidden — grey on grey, the occasional suggestion of a ridgeline dissolving before it could take shape. Disappointing, but not devastating. Pokhara has a way of making up for what the sky withholds.

### The Pokhara Cab Situation

A word on ground transport: InDrive, the ride-hailing app that works well enough in Kathmandu, proved useless at Pokhara's airport. The driver refused to come inside, asking us to walk a kilometre out to the highway instead. We did what any sensible traveller does when faced with that kind of negotiation — took the cab parked right outside. Twelve hundred rupees on the meter, no haggling, no walking. Expensive by local standards, but the luxury of not caring was worth every rupee.

### Majestic Lakeview Front

The hotel more than made up for the cab fare. We checked into Majestic Lakeview Front and were immediately upgraded to a lake-view room — early check-in, no fuss, no extra charge. The room had a bathtub and a wall of glass that opened onto Phewa Lake and, beyond it, the full sweep of the Annapurna range. The kind of view you'd pay triple for in Switzerland, handed to us like it was nothing.

### Breakfast in the Rain

We headed down for a late breakfast as the rain picked up again. Steamed chicken momos — delicate, the filling juicy and well-spiced — and a pesto chicken sandwich that had no business being that good at a lakeside hotel. I ordered a hot Americano, and here's something I've noticed about Nepali coffee culture: they always ask whether you want a single or double shot, every single time, with genuine investment in your answer. And they use far less water than you'd get anywhere in India. The result is a punchy, concentrated cup that feels more espresso-adjacent than Americano. No complaints.

### The Pokhara Circuit — Four Stops, One Cab

We booked a car through the hotel for 3,500 rupees to cover the tourist circuit: Devi's Falls, Gupteshwor Cave, Pumdikot Shiva Temple, and the World Peace Pagoda. A full afternoon.

**Devi's Falls** came first. The story goes that a Swiss woman named Mrs. Davis was swept away and drowned here when the river surged without warning — the falls now bear a Nepali-ised version of her name. As a waterfall, it's modest. The water drops into a narrow gorge and disappears underground, more geological curiosity than spectacle. Respectfully, Mrs. Davis — the legacy deserves a more dramatic cascade.

**Gupteshwor Cave**, just across the road, was the better half of that pairing. The descent is long and narrow, the stone walls pressing in as you go deeper, the air turning thick and damp. There are moments where the passage tightens enough to trigger a flicker of claustrophobia — bodies squeezing past each other, water dripping from unseen crevices, the heat building with every step down. But the final chamber opens up to a view of the falls from inside the earth, water thundering into rock, and suddenly the entire sweaty descent makes sense. Coming back out into open air felt like surfacing from a dive.

**Pumdikot** required a drive uphill that was, to put it diplomatically, character-building. The road narrowed and steepened with each switchback, the kind of gradient where you stop looking out the window and start studying the driver's hands instead. The Shiva temple at the top is striking — a massive statue visible from across the valley — though neither of us being particularly religious, we did a respectful loop and moved on to the real draw: a cup of tandoori chai from a stall near the viewpoint. Smoky, sweet, served in a clay kulhar, the kind of chai that tastes better because of where you're standing.

**The World Peace Pagoda** was the final stop, and the best. The driver dropped us at the top of a ridge, from where it's a short off-road trek — about a kilometre — down to the pagoda. The walk itself was half the experience: dirt paths cutting through forest, the valley opening up in glimpses between the trees, Phewa Lake glinting far below. The pagoda is a Japanese-built Buddhist stupa, white and symmetrical and deliberately serene. While we stood there, a monk emerged and began a slow circumambulation, beating a hand drum in a steady, unhurried rhythm. The sound carried across the hilltop and something in it — the repetition, the simplicity — made the noise in your head go quiet for a moment. That's the best way I can describe it.

### The Lake Temple and the Walk Back

The driver dropped us near Lakeside at the entrance to Tal Barahi Temple — a small shrine that sits on an island in the middle of Phewa Lake. A short boat ride across, a quick darshan, and we were peering over the railings at fat, languid fish circling below the surface. It's a modest place, but the setting — water on all sides, mountains behind, the gentle rock of the boat — gives it a weight that the architecture alone wouldn't.

We walked back to the hotel along the lakeshore parapets, and this is where Pokhara surprised me most. Lakeside has the unmistakable energy of a beach town — shacks with thatched roofs, fairy lights, music drifting from open-air bars, backpackers in flip-flops browsing shops selling singing bowls and tie-dye. It's Goa energy at 800 metres above sea level, surrounded by snow-capped peaks. The cognitive dissonance is delightful.

### Bamboo Boat and the Altitude Effect

Shriya spotted the place on the walk back — Bamboo Boat, a lakeside shack with low seating and the right kind of lighting. We dropped our bags at the hotel and came straight back.

Three large Barhasinghe beers. Barbecued sticks of chicken and pork, charred and smoky. Kurkure chicken — crispy fried pieces with a spice coating that existed purely to justify another sip. And wai wai sadheko — those instant noodles, crushed and tossed with onion, chilli, lime, and mustard oil, the ultimate Nepali bar snack, engineered for exactly this purpose. The chakna cravings were comprehensively addressed.

Here's what nobody warns you about: beer at altitude hits different. Pokhara sits at roughly 800 metres, and whether it's the thin air, the exhaustion, or just the mountain magic, three beers here felt like five on flat ground. We were, to use the technical term, fairly drunk — happy, loose-limbed, and suddenly in urgent need of dessert. An ice cream shop nearby obliged: a raspberry cone for one, an ice cream sandwich for the other. The walk back to the hotel was short, wobbly, and entirely content.

We were asleep within minutes.

## Day Four — Sarangkot and the Descent
*March 22, 2026*

### 5 AM, No Excuses

The alarm went off at five. After last night's Barhasinghe-fuelled adventures, this felt like a personal attack — but Sarangkot waits for no one, and the sunrise window is unforgiving. We were out the door by 5:30, bundled into a cab the hotel had arranged the night before. The streets of Pokhara were empty, the air sharp and cold, the sky still holding on to the last of its darkness.

### The Stairway to Heaven

The cab dropped us at the Sarangkot viewpoint, and within the first flight of stairs the altitude made itself known. Pokhara may sit at 800 metres, but Sarangkot climbs to nearly 1,600 — and our lungs, still calibrated for Bangalore's sea-level comfort, protested immediately. A few flights up, we were breathing like we'd sprinted a mile. The body humbles you at height.

But the view — the view obliterated every complaint. The Annapurna massif spread across the northern horizon in a wall of white, peaks only a kilometre shorter than Everest itself, their summits so high they seemed to belong to a different atmosphere entirely. Snow blazed orange and pink in the early light, the kind of colour that doesn't look real even when you're staring directly at it. Below, a carpet of white cloud filled the valley floor, masking Phewa Lake entirely, so that the mountains appeared to rise not from the earth but from an ocean of mist. We stood above the clouds, looking up at the Himalayas, and for a few minutes nobody said anything at all. Stairway to heaven isn't a metaphor here. It's a description.

### The Trek Down

We had planned this part in advance: rather than cab back, we'd walk down to the hotel on foot. The descent began on steep, rocky stairs slick with fallen leaves — the kind where you plant each foot carefully and hope your knees hold the bargain. The stone steps gave way, eventually, to narrow dirt pathways that hugged the hillside, forest pressing in from both sides, birdsong replacing the silence of the summit.

Every now and then, the canopy would break and the entire city of Pokhara would appear below — the lake a long silver mirror, the Lakeside strip visible as a thin line of rooftops, the valley floor impossibly far down. These glimpses kept us moving when the legs started to argue.

The final forty-five minutes cut through dense forest — dark, quiet, the trail soft underfoot, shafts of light filtering through the trees like something out of a Miyazaki film. Beautiful, but by this point our knees had filed a formal grievance. The wobble was real. When we finally hit paved road and saw the outskirts of the city, the relief was physical.

We walked the last stretch back to the hotel in silence — the good kind, the kind that comes from having earned your tiredness. Checked straight in, collapsed into the breakfast room, and let the hotel buffet do its healing work.

### Fresh Juicery and the Goa of the Himalayas

After showers and checkout, we wandered into Fresh Juicery — a café that could have been lifted wholesale from Anjuna. The same energy: white expats draped over cushioned seats, the mingling haze of coffee and cigarette smoke, racks of expensive Indian-print clothing by the entrance. If you've been to Artjuna in Goa, you know the exact vibe.

We ordered a hummus platter with flatbread — the bread arriving the size of a roomali roti, thin and floppy and perfect for tearing. An Americano and the café's signature latte followed. Then, in a moment of misplaced confidence, I ordered the Himalayan salt cream coffee — a drink that sounded poetic and tasted fine, right up until my longstanding feud with lactose reasserted itself. Some lessons you learn more than once.

### The Great Restaurant Hunt

What followed was a comedy of errors that every traveller knows too well. A friend had recommended a place — Turkish and Nepalese Kitchen — where Shriya was hoping to find a proper Thakali set. We arrived to discover neither a Thakali set on the menu nor a waiter anywhere in sight. We left as quickly as we'd sat down.

Shriya found a second option. We walked over, sat down, opened the menu — and it had changed entirely. No Thakali set. I ordered chicken momos on principle, because at this point you eat what's available and keep moving. Eventually, a third restaurant nearby did have the Thakali set — a chicken version served with roti instead of rice. Shriya was finally appeased. The hunt had taken longer than the meal itself, but that's Pokhara.

### Three-Hour Delay and the Purple Haze Payoff

We reached the airport by three, bags packed, spirits high, ready for the short hop back to Kathmandu. The flight had other plans — delayed by three hours. We sat, we waited, we watched the departure board with the dead-eyed patience that frequent flyers develop as a survival mechanism. We finally landed at Tribhuvan at 8:15 PM, which meant Pashupatinath Temple and its evening aarti — the one thing we'd planned for the return — was off the table.

Straight to the hotel. Bags down. Back out.

Purple Haze had been circled on the mental itinerary since Friday night, and it did not disappoint. The bar delivered exactly what it had promised through those thumping doors: live Nepali rock music — a band tearing through covers and originals with the kind of energy that makes you forget you've been sitting in an airport for three hours. We settled in with beer, chicken choila — smoky and fierce as ever — and a plate of cheesy fries that existed purely as ballast. The room was loud, the crowd was in it, and Thamel's Sunday night proved it doesn't need a weekend to justify a good time.

We left at 10:15, buzzing, and went looking for proper dinner — only to discover that Thamel's kitchens close earlier than its bars. Most places had already shut their grills. We eventually found a resort restaurant still serving, ordered chicken fried rice and chicken noodles — above average, nothing more, nothing less — and called it a night. Kathmandu's last evening had delivered.

## Day Five — Bhaktapur, Finally, and the Road Home
*March 23, 2026*

### The Unfinished Business

We'd been rained out of Bhaktapur on Day Two — a hailstorm had turned us back at the halfway point, and the ancient city had been sitting on the to-do list ever since. Today, we'd finish what we started.

Up at six. Out by seven. The cab cut through Kathmandu's early-morning traffic — lighter on a Sunday, the city still yawning — and deposited us at Bhaktapur's entrance within the hour.

### A Guide Best Left Unnamed

We hired a guide at the gate. This turned out to be the one misstep of the trip. The man offered little in the way of history or context, seemed personally offended that we weren't interested in performing prayers at every temple we passed — and by the hundredth shrine, we'd passed quite a few — and spent a suspicious amount of time steering us toward specific shops where, one imagines, a commission awaited. We gave him his promised thousand rupees, thanked him with more grace than he'd earned, and set off on our own. Bhaktapur deserved better narration, and honestly, so did we.

### Bhaktapur on Our Own Terms

Freed from the guide, we found breakfast at Himalayan Java Coffee — a chain we'd been spotting all across Nepal, their green logo as ubiquitous as Starbucks in Seattle. My stomach, still nursing the salt cream coffee betrayal from Pokhara, called for a watermelon lemon cooler. Shriya had a cappuccino. We split the signature sandwich and a Mexican omelette — both solid, the kind of reliable café food that gets you back on your feet.

The real discovery came on the way out: King Curd, or Juju Dhau — Bhaktapur's legendary yogurt, famous enough to have its own mythology. Served cold in a clay pot, baked and set, it was almost indistinguishable from Bengali mishti doi — thick, sweet, with a caramelised skin on top. As a dessert and a digestive, it was exactly what the morning needed.

We spent the rest of the morning exploring properly — the 55-Window Palace with its extraordinary woodwork, and then the Bhaktapur Art Museum, which turned out to be a genuinely impressive collection. Paubha paintings, bronze sculptures, intricate woodcarvings — the craftsmanship on display was remarkable. A sidenote for the unprepared: expect a generous selection of what the museum diplomatically labels "yogic postures" — Kamasutra-adjacent artwork rendered with the same devotional precision as the deities flanking them. Nepal doesn't separate the sacred from the sensual, and neither does its art.

### Pashupatinath — The Final Temple

From Bhaktapur, we pointed the cab toward Pashupatinath — the holiest Hindu temple in Nepal, the one site we couldn't leave without visiting. The welcome committee, however, was less than holy. Within seconds of stepping out of the cab, a man materialised at our side, offering directions with the practiced warmth of someone who does this fifty times a day. He walked us toward what he claimed was the entrance but turned out to be a side gate — conveniently adjacent to his shop. More of the same followed: helpful strangers who wanted nothing more than to guide us, each one orbiting a different storefront. We declined politely, repeatedly, and found the proper entrance on our own.

We deposited our shoes at the designated counter and entered the temple premises. Pashupatinath is vast — sprawling ghats along the Bagmati River, stone shrines in every direction, sadhus in saffron, the smell of incense and woodsmoke. We walked the grounds but chose not to enter the inner sanctum — the queues were long, the cremation pyres along the river were a sobering reminder of why people come here, and our flight was ticking closer. Some places reveal themselves fully only when you have the time to sit with them. Pashupatinath, we decided, deserves a return trip.

The monkeys, meanwhile, had no such philosophical patience. They patrolled the exit path like seasoned muggers, lunging at anyone carrying food with a boldness that made Swayambhunath's macaques look amateur. We dodged, weaved, and made it to the cab with our belongings intact — a small victory to end the trip.

### Homeward

Tribhuvan International Airport. Check-in. Security. The familiar ritual of leaving a place that got under your skin faster than you expected. Five days in Nepal — Kathmandu's chaos and charm, Pokhara's impossible beauty, Bhaktapur's quiet grandeur — and the feeling wasn't that we'd seen it all. It was that we'd barely scratched the surface.

The flight banked south over the valley, and for a brief moment, the clouds parted. A flash of white peaks on the horizon — the mountains offering one last glimpse, as if to say: you'll be back.

We landed in Bangalore. The humidity hit like a wall. The cab smelled of air freshener. The roads were flat. Corporate life resumed, as it always does, but somewhere in the luggage — between the dirty clothes and the half-eaten packet of crispy corn — Nepal was still there, waiting to be unpacked.
