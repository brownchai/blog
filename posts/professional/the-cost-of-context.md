Paul Graham wrote that the enemy of creative work is interruption. That was in 2009. The problem has only gotten worse.

## The 23-minute myth

Research often cited in engineering circles suggests it takes 23 minutes to recover full concentration after an interruption. I don't know if that number is exact, but I know the direction is correct — and that it undersells the problem.

The issue isn't just recovering attention. It's recovering *context*. When you're deep in a debugging session, you have a mental model loaded: the call stack, the variables you've inspected, the hypotheses you've ruled out. An interruption doesn't just pull your eyes off the screen. It evicts that model.

Loading it back takes longer than 23 minutes if the problem is hard enough.

## Why meetings are expensive on the wrong schedule

A meeting in the middle of a morning doesn't cost one hour. It costs the two focused blocks on either side of it, because you can't commit to a long problem you know you'll have to drop.

Makers and managers have fundamentally different time requirements. Managers need to be interruptible — that's part of the job. Makers need to be uninterruptible for long enough to build something.

The mistake organisations make is scheduling makers on a manager's calendar.

## What high-leverage focus looks like

The engineers I've learned the most from share a common trait: they're unreachable when they're working. Not permanently — they're responsive in the mornings and responsive at end of day. But between certain hours, they're gone.

This isn't rudeness. It's respect for the work.

## Small things that help

- **Async by default.** If something can wait two hours for a response, it should. Most things can.
- **Batch communication.** Check messages at fixed times, not continuously.
- **Protect mornings.** The first hours of the day are cognitively richest for most people. Don't give them to email.
- **Declare focus publicly.** Telling your team when you're in a focus block normalises the practice.

The goal isn't to be unavailable. It's to be available at the right times for the right things — and to do the hard thinking when you're actually capable of it.
